Raptors

  • Families
    • Falconiformes:
      • Diurnal Birds
      • Hawks, Falcons, Eagles, Vultures
      • have a crop, and a vestigial cecum
    • Strigiformes
      • Mostly nocturnal, a few are diurnal
      • Owls
      • Females generally larger
      • no crop but well developed cecum
  • Handling
    • Generally if you can control the feet, you will have control of the bird (exception: small falcons, owls, all vultures)
    • Always have eye protection, thick gloves and a towel
    • remember not to compress the sternum, bird cant breathe if you do.
  • Release Criteria
    • suitable time of year and location
    • suitable BCS
    • normal hunting skills
    • normal feather condition
    • normal vision
    • 100% return to function (legs, wings, etc)
  • Poor prognosis for release
    • Open fx near joints
    • luxation/subluxations
    • Osteomyelitis
    • Loss of foot, hallux or multiple toes
    • blind eye / imperfect vision
    • imprinting or other behavioral issues

Babies and Juveniles

+ Metabolic bone disease can arise with 5-15 days of poor feeding
+ Imprinting occurs quickly, never feed animal without using a mask and puppet.
+ Juvenile cataracts is normal. termed 'blue eye', adaptation to prevent injury to eyes while around the period where they learn to fly. Temporary condition, dont remove eyes because you think its cataracts in young raptor.

Diseases

### Lead
    + Lead toxicity is secondary to ingestion of lead. Typically from lead shot used by hunters in prey species.
    + Cannot get lead toxicity by being shot, only from having lead in the stomach
    + Blood lead levels
        - Normal: 0.2-0.4 ppm
        - Elevated: > 0.5 ppm
        - Toxic Signs: > 1 ppm
        - Poor prognosis: > 2 ppm
    + Treatment
        - Supportive care
        - removal of lead (sx, endoscopy, gastric lavage, encourage passing of cast)
        - Chelation therapy: Calcium disodium versenate (calcium EDTA), D-penicillamine, Dimercaptosuccinic acid (DMSA/dimercaperol)
### Pododermatitis (bumblefoot)
    + Caused by a ascending bacterial infection of the feet. Typically Staphylococcus aureus
    + Most common in falcons, or heavy birds which perch for long periods of time
    + Etiology: improperly shaped perches, inappropriate housing, unsanitary cages, dietary/vitamin deficiencies, overgrown talons, obesity, basically anything that places more weight or pressure points on the bottom of the foot.
    + Classifications
        - Type 4: Epithelial thinning, loss of epithelium. Tx: clean feet, correct environemnt
        - Type 3: Discrete hyperkeratinized pad lesion. Tx: Topical Abx and bandaging. (DMSO + topical Abx work well)
        - Type 2: Discrete encapsulated lesion of metatarsal pad. Tx: surgery
        - Type 1: Cellulitis and gross enlargement of meta-tarsal pad, osteomyelitis, arthritis. Tx: surgery; healing time often prolonged
### Avian tuberculosis (M. avium)
    + Bacterial infection
    + Clinical signs: weakness, chronic weight loss, exertional dyspnea, non-specific signs
    + Diagnosis: Leukocytosis w/ monocytosis, radiographic changes including hepatosplenomegaly, granulomas, bone marrow masses), positive acid fast feces stain or biopsy and culture. Skin tests are inconclusive
    + Treatment: Quarantine, replace bedding, strict sanitation
### Raptor Herpes Virus
    + Most common in falcons and eagles, invariably fatal
    + Clinical Signs: Wakness and anorexia 1-2 days before death. acute death
    + Histopath: eosinophilic, intranuclear inclusion bodies in the liver
    + Diagnosis: Herpesvirus isolation
    + Treatment: None; quarantine or euthanize
#### Aspergillosis
    + Fungal infection: most important disease of raptors
    + Common in Bald eagles, Golden Eagles, Goshawks, Gyrfalcons, Peregrine falcons
    + Clinical Signs: Often none until in advanced stages; dyspnea, exercise intolerance, weight loss, voice changes
    + Diagnosis: Radiographs, tracheal or air sac wash, culture, endoscope, ELISA, leukocytosis
    + Treatment: Amphotericin B, Flucytosine, Itraconazole
### Candidiasis
    + Fungal infection: often secondary to long term antibiotic therapy
    + Clinical Signs: Psuedomembrane/plaque in mouth, crop, upper GI system
    + Diagnosis: Cytology of yeast like organisms
    + Treatment: Nystatin, Ketoconazole, Itraconazole
#### Parasites
    + External: feather lice, hippoboscid flys
    + Nematodes: Capillaria, Serratospiculinum
    + Coccidia: common in juveniles
    + Protozoans: Trichomonas (fungal plaques in mouth - treat with metronidazole)
    + Hemoparasites: Hemoproteus (considered non-pathogenic), Leucocytozoon (may be pathogenic), Plasmodium (malaria)
