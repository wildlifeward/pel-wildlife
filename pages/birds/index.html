Birds

  • Fluid Maintenance
    • Avians: 5-10% of body weight BID (50-120 ml/kg); smaller birds have higher fluid requirements
    • Specifically for crystalloids: Daily fluid maintenance = BWkg0.75 * k
      • K = 78 for non passerines | K = 129 for passerines
      • Typically give 50% of daily maintenance SQ Q12h

  • Feeding

    • Minimum energy costs (MEC) = Kcal/day a resting animal needs
      • MEC = Kvalue * Kg0.75
      • Kvalues are as follows
        • Passerines: 129
        • Non-passerines: 78
      • Multiply MEC value as follows
        • young/growing animal: MEC * 2-3
        • Moderately injured adult: MEC * 2
        • Severely injured adult: MEC * 3
        • Sick Juvenile: MEC * 3-4
    • Start with 3% of body weight BID and increase as needed
    • Pigeons/Doves: Start at 5% body weight BID and increase as needed. Most adult pigeons can ingest 15ml at a time

  • Obtaining Blood

    • Blood volume of birds is ~ 6-10% of body weight. 10% of blood volume can be taken safely in most healthy birds. SO - max amount of blood in mLs to take safely is 1% of body weight (grams). ie: 1 Kg bird you can take 10mL max. 100g bird you can take 1ml max.
    • EDTA is NOT a suitable anticoagulant for birds, use a lithium heparin tube (green top)
    • Areas to draw blood:
      • Medial Metatarsal Vein: Site of choice for blood draws as is it surrounded by muscle which reduces chances of a hematoma forming. In raptors, this vein is often best visualized just proximal to the medial aspect of the hock joint.
      • Cutaneous ulnar vein (Basilar vein): runs across the ventral surface of the wing, over the ulna, just distal to elbow joint directly under the skin. Hematomas often result from blood collection at this site.
      • Jugular vein: Use the right jugular as the left is generally very small. A featherless tract of skin can be found overlying the right jugular in most species. Jugular is the site of choice for small birds IF a maximum amount of blood is needed. Very large hematomas at this site can occur as the vein is very movable and there is a large amount of SQ space in this area.
    • Making a blood smear
      • Best method for bird blood (which is more fragile than mammal blood):
        • 1) Place a small, round drop of blood on a slide with a non-heparinized capillary tube.
        • 2) Gently drop cover glass onto blood, parallel to slide.
        • 3) Wait until blood radiates out between slide and coverglass ( ̃1 second).
        • 4) Evenly pull slide and cover glass apart horizontally--do not separate or lift up while pulling. The blood smear should be thin (“rainbow” effect of various color or hues along the feathered edges of the smear).
        • 5) Air dry.
        • 6) Stain as soon as slide dries.
