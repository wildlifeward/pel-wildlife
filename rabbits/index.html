Rabbits

Cottontail Rabbits

  • Baby Bunnies are very difficult to rehab.. Mom is much better at taking care and raising them than humans can. If rabbit is 'stolen' best bet for it is returning it to where it was found. This is becuase mom provides gut microbes to her babies through 'cecotrophs'. without these cecotrophs, the baby rabbits dont develop the gut bacteria they need to digest their food and die from malnutrition
  • Babies on ground
    • Average litter size is 5 rabbits but can have up to 12 young. Nests are shallow depressions in the ground lined by grass and/or rabbit fur
    • Baby rabbits (once eyes are open and furred) are left by themselves usually in small divets in the ground. The mother visits these areas 2-4x/day to feed the bunnies, typically each feeding takes ~ 30 seconds. Dusk and dawn are most common times the mom feeds her young. Then mother goes away to keep predators from finding babies
    • To tell if baby bunnies are being fed by mom; place a few pieces of yarn over the babies in the nest in a crisscross pattern. If by morning the yarn is moved then you know the mom has been feeding her young
    • Young rabbits are on their own at ~ 3 weeks of age (about size of a softball, > 100g). They stay in the nest until 15-20 days.

  • Use Goats milk in the following: 1 part powdered goat milk:2 parts warm-hot water (bunnies like their milk very warm, almost hot). Can add 1/4 part heavy whipping cream in to more closely approximate moms milk nutrients.

  • For the first feeding start with 1 part formula above: 2-3 parts water. Over next few feedings gradually decrease added water. Feeding in this way prevents diarrhea from new food being fed

  • Feeding

    • Rule of thumb: give rabbits max 10% of body weight if syringe feeding. ie: 70g rabbit gets 7cc fluid/replacer 3-4x/day.

    • NEVER feed Iceberg lettuce - gums up their digestive tract and has no nutritive value. Use dandelions, any natural grasses (with roots and some dirt still attached), clover and wildflowers (stems and flowers)
    • Alfalfa hay is fine in small amounts, timothy hay or mixed grass hay is best long term for them. Alfalfa is really high in calories and calcium.

  • Medication

    • Rabbits have well established anaerobes living in their cecum and colon that helps keep their GI normal. They get dysbiosis when we treat them with drugs that wipe out these 'good bacteria'. Stay away from gram-positive and anaerobic spectrum drugs. ie: (penicillins, lincosamides (clindamycin, erythromycin), gentamycin, cephalosporins) PLACE Penicillin, Lincosamide, amoxicillin, cephalosporins, clindamycin, erythromycin)
    • Cat bites: Make sure well hydrated before meds. TMS (trimethoprimsulfa) at 30mg/kg. Enrofloxacin (careful in young animals). Dual-pen seems to be ok for them parenteraly once a week but do not use clavamox.
      • Meloxicam 0.5mg/kg PO BID
      • Tramadol 5mg/kg PO BID
      • TMS 30mg/kg PO BID
      • SSD: thin layer to wounds, stop after 3 days.
    • Release
    • Release during good weather for the next few days (not raining, windy, etc)
    • Release at no less than 120 grams (~6-8 weeks old)
